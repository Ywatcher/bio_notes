# Engineering of Primary Human B cells with CRISPR/Cas9 Targeted Nuclease

Source and citation key: \cite{Johnson2018EngineeringPrimaryHumanBCells}

Why this source was read: foundational tooling paper underlying most of the other papers in the `b_cell_engineering_2026-07-16` bucket, promoted after full text became available.

## What was engineered?

Primary human B cells isolated from blood, activated in culture, then edited using CRISPR/Cas9 delivered either as a ready-made protein-RNA complex (RNP) or as mRNA.

## Main engineering decision

Work out the practical cell-handling and delivery conditions — timing after activation, electroporation settings, RNP versus mRNA delivery of Cas9 — that make any downstream B-cell editing project actually feasible.

## Scale map

Intervention at the molecular/methods level. Consequences appear at the cellular level: viability, expansion, and editing rate, rather than any specific therapeutic outcome.

## Main measurement

Delivering Cas9 as a protein-RNA complex clearly outperformed delivering it as mRNA, both for editing efficiency and for how well cells survived and expanded afterward.

## Dominant constraint

Keeping cells alive and dividing after editing — the mRNA delivery route appeared to trigger the cell's own innate viral-sensing defenses, which the protein-based route avoided.

## Closest comparison

\cite{Laoharawee2020CRISPRPrimaryHumanBCells} — a later protocol paper from the same lab building on this method (full text not available for independent verification here).

## One thing learned

How the editing machinery is delivered, not just which sequence is targeted, can determine whether cells survive to become useful for anything downstream.

## Open questions

Does this delivery lesson (RNP over mRNA) generalize cleanly to the locus-editing strategies in the newer papers in this bucket, or did those need further protocol adjustments specific to their target loci?
