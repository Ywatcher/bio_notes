# A precision gene-engineered B cell medicine producing sustained levels of active factor IX for hemophilia B therapy

Source and citation key: \cite{Liu2025EngineeredBCellFactorIX}

Why this source was read: candidate in the `b_cell_engineering_2026-07-16` rough-sources bucket, explicitly noted as not BCR-specificity engineering but direct B-cell/plasma-cell protein-delivery engineering; promoted after full text became available.

## What was engineered?

Patient-derived B cells, edited and differentiated into plasma cells used purely as a protein-manufacturing platform, not for BCR specificity.

## Main engineering decision

Insert the therapeutic Factor IX transgene at a genomic safe-harbor site (CCR5) rather than at the immunoglobulin locus, since the goal is steady long-term secretion rather than receptor specificity.

## Scale map

Intervention at a genomic site unrelated to the rearrangement pathway entirely. Consequences are measured at the organism level — circulating therapeutic protein levels over months.

## Main measurement

Therapeutic protein was detectable within a day of treatment, remained at stable and biologically active levels for well over six months, and a second dose produced a further proportional increase in circulating levels.

## Dominant constraint

How many of the engineered cells actually engraft and persist long-term — the authors could only estimate this roughly in their animal model.

## Closest comparison

\cite{Hill2026EngineeredPlasmaCellBiology} — both use engineered plasma cells as secretion platforms, though that paper still touches the antibody locus for its screening step.

## One thing learned

Not every "B cell engineering" project touches the rearrangement machinery at all — this one only borrows the plasma cell's natural talent for long-term, high-volume protein secretion.

## Open questions

How does engrafting engineered cells at a safe-harbor locus compare, in long-term durability, to approaches that instead engineer the endogenous antibody locus directly?
