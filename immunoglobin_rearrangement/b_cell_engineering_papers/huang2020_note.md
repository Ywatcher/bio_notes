# Vaccine elicitation of HIV broadly neutralizing antibodies from engineered B cells

Source and citation key: \cite{Huang2020VaccineElicitationEngineeredBCells}

Why this source was read: candidate in the `b_cell_engineering_2026-07-16` rough-sources bucket; companion approach to \cite{Nahmad2020EngineeredBCellsAntiHIV}, promoted to its own note after full text became available.

## What was engineered?

Mouse B cells edited with CRISPR/Cas9 to insert the VRC01 anti-HIV broadly neutralizing antibody, comparing two strategies: editing the heavy-chain locus alone versus editing both heavy and kappa light-chain loci separately.

## Main engineering decision

Test whether inserting both chains independently (for a fully defined receptor) works better than inserting only the heavy chain and letting it pair with the cell's own endogenous light chain.

## Scale map

Intervention at the locus level (two variants). Consequences appear at the cellular level (expansion, viability) and at the organism/serum level (antibody titers tracked over roughly seven months).

## Main measurement

The heavy-chain-only strategy produced a durable, measurable serum antibody response after vaccination, with evidence of ongoing mutation in the inserted sequence. The dual-locus strategy, despite comparable editing success, produced no detectable antibody response at all.

## Dominant constraint

Functional receptor assembly and pairing — successfully editing both loci did not translate into a working immune response, revealing a bottleneck between "edited" and "functional" that isn't visible from the editing efficiency alone.

## Closest comparison

\cite{Nahmad2020EngineeredBCellsAntiHIV} — same overall aim, different knock-in design.

## One thing learned

Successful gene editing at the intended locus does not guarantee a functional downstream immune response; there can be a hidden failure mode between "the DNA change worked" and "the cell does its job."

## Open questions

What specifically causes the dual-locus strategy to fail — receptor chain pairing, cell-intrinsic selection against a fully synthetic receptor, or something upstream of both?
